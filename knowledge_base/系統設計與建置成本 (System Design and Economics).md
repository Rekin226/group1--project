# Aquaponics System Architecture and Economic Cost Analysis

This technical engineering guide outlines the primary aquaponics system architectures, their functional mechanics, and the associated economic variables required for commercial-scale viability.

### Deep Water Culture (DWC) / Raft System
#### Description & Function
- Serves as the commercial benchmark for mass production of leafy greens due to high environmental buffering.
- Utilizes expansive raceways (e.g., 29.6m x 1.3m x 0.4m) requiring structural reinforcement for approximately 110 cubic meters of water.
- Employs 3.8cm food-grade EPS foam rafts and high-output blowers to maintain dissolved oxygen above 5 mg/L.
- Requires a balanced feed input ratio of 57 to 100 grams of feed per square meter per day.
#### Pros & Cons
- Provides massive thermal stability, acting as a heat sink to protect root zones in volatile climates.
- High operational risk of fine solids accumulation; if not filtered, particulates can create anaerobic "dead zones" and produce $H_2S$.
- Identified as the primary revenue driver, though it incurs high recurring electricity costs for continuous aeration.
#### Economic Variables
- **HDPE Liner (1.0 mm)**: $1.50 - $3.50 per sq meter (Expected lifespan: 20-30 years).
- **EPS Foam Rafts**: $21.00 - $31.00 per raft (Expected lifespan: 5-10 years).
- **Aeration System (1.5 hp)**: $1,200 - $1,800 per system (Expected lifespan: 3-5 years).
- **Concrete Raceway**: $18.00 - $28.00 per linear foot (Expected lifespan: 40+ years).

### Nutrient Film Technique (NFT)
#### Description & Function
- Ideal for space-constrained environments like rooftops or vertical farms where weight is limited.
- Features 10cm x 7cm food-grade PVC channels with a nutrient film flow rate of 1 to 2 liters per minute.
- Requires precise gravitational slope alignment between 1:100 and 1:30 for optimal root oxygenation.
#### Pros & Cons
- Highly space-efficient but acutely vulnerable to mechanical or power failures.
- Lacks thermal mass, causing water temperatures to fluctuate rapidly with ambient air.
- Clogging risks: Large fruiting plant roots can block narrow channels, leading to overflows and crop loss.
#### Economic Variables
- **Food-grade PVC Channel**: $5.50 - $11.50 per linear meter (Expected lifespan: 10-15 years).
- **Support Rack (Steel/Aluminum)**: $25.00 - $45.00 per linear meter (Expected lifespan: 20+ years).
- **Nutrient Manifold (LDPE)**: $2.50 - $4.00 per channel (Expected lifespan: 5-7 years).
- **Water Pump (1/8 hp)**: $150 - $350 per system (Expected lifespan: 2-4 years).

### Media-Based Grow Beds
#### Description & Function
- Provides foundational stability for "top-heavy" crops like tomatoes or peppers.
- Engineered to a 30cm depth to balance biofiltration volume and adequate root space.
- Utilizes bell siphons to drive flood-and-drain cycles based on the Bernoulli principle.
#### Pros & Cons
- Enables mineralization where heterotrophic bacteria convert solid waste into inorganic nutrients.
- Structural challenge: Wet media beds exert extreme static loads exceeding 500 kg per square meter.
- Media choice impacts labor: River gravel is cheaper but increases clogging risk and labor intensity compared to Hydroton (LECA).
#### Economic Variables
- **Hydroton (LECA)**: $350 - $550 per cubic meter (Expected lifespan: Indefinite).
- **River Gravel (3/4 inch)**: $40 - $85 per cubic meter (Expected lifespan: Indefinite).
- **IBC Grow Bed (Modified)**: $85 - $150 per unit (Expected lifespan: 10-15 years).
- **Bell Siphon Assembly**: $35 - $65 per unit (Expected lifespan: 5-10 years).

### Solid Waste Filtration (Clarifiers & Separators)
#### Description & Function
- Mechanical filtration enabling high-density fish stocking of 60-70 kg per 1,000L.
- Uses cylindro-conical clarifiers or swirl separators to remove solids via sedimentation or centrifugal force.
- UVI configurations include four net filter tanks for fine particulate trapping and nitrogen mineralization.
#### Pros & Cons
- Critical for preventing root coating and maintaining dissolved oxygen levels.
- Major cost center: Specialized filtration and tanks comprise approximately $15,170 of total equipment costs.
- Maintenance intensive: Daily purging of sludge results in a 1.5% daily water loss.
#### Economic Variables
- **Conical Clarifier (1,000 Gal)**: $2,155 per unit (Expected lifespan: 20+ years).
- **Swirl Separator (500 Gal)**: $1,000 per unit (Expected lifespan: 20+ years).
- **Net Filter Tank (185 Gal)**: $805 per unit (Expected lifespan: 20+ years).
- **Purge Ball Valve (3 inch)**: $65 - $115 per unit (Expected lifespan: 5-10 years).

### Biological Filtration (Biofilters)
#### Description & Function
- The biochemical engine converting toxic ammonia into nitrate ($NO_3^-N$).
- Stoichiometry: Consumes 4.3 mg of oxygen and 7.1 mg of alkalinity for every 1 mg of ammonia oxidized.
- Utilizes Moving Bed Biofilm Reactors (MBBR) to maximize Specific Surface Area (SSA) for bacteria.
#### Pros & Cons
- MBBR systems are self-cleaning due to media collision, reducing manual labor costs.
- High-SSA media (like K+ Advanced) allows for smaller footprints compared to traditional media.
- Operational risk: Nitrification is acidifying and can cause pH crashes if not monitored.
#### Economic Variables
- **K1 Kaldnes Media (500 $m^2/m^3$ SSA)**: $300 - $700 per cubic meter (Expected lifespan: 15-20 years).
- **K+ Advanced Media (1,025 $m^2/m^3$ SSA)**: $750 - $1,100 per cubic meter (Expected lifespan: 15-20 years).
- **Bio-Balls (220 $m^2/m^3$ SSA)**: $150 - $250 per cubic meter (Expected lifespan: 10-15 years).
- **Linear Air Blower (80-120 LPM)**: $250 - $450 per unit (Expected lifespan: 3-5 years).

### Agricultural Economic Synthesis
#### Summary of Economics
- **Total Capital Investment**: Approximately $33,700 is required for a commercial-scale UVI/FAO model.
- **Cost Structure**: Filtration and aquaculture are the highest investments ($15,170), enabling the revenue-generating DWC subsystem ($11,622).
- **Performance Benchmark**: System success is measured by a target Feed Conversion Ratio (FCR) of 1.76.
- **Regulatory Risks**: Local restrictions (e.g., Tilapia bans in South Africa) can impact species selection and profitability margins.
