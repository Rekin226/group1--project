# Aquaponics Fish Health and Disease Management

**Mandatory System Safety Protocol**: The following substances are strictly prohibited for use within the main aquaponic system due to their lethal effects on nitrifying bacteria (biofilter) and potential for phytotoxicity or bioaccumulation in edible plant tissues:
- **Copper sulfate**: Highly toxic to plants and beneficial microbes.
- **Methylene Blue**: Disrupts biological filtration and causes permanent staining.
- **Systemic Antibiotics**: Kills the nitrifying biofilter and creates risks of antibiotic-resistant bacteria in food crops.

---

### Ichthyophthirius multifiliis (White Spot Disease / Ich)
#### Symptoms
- Affected fish exhibit characteristic small, white, raised cysts (trophonts) resembling grains of salt on the skin, fins, and gills.
- Behavioral changes include "flashing" or rubbing against tank surfaces to alleviate irritation, clamped fins, and respiratory distress if the gills are heavily infested.
#### Root Cause / Pathogen
- Caused by a large ciliated protozoan parasite with a complex, temperature-dependent lifecycle (trophont, tomont, and theronts).
- Disease is typically triggered by sudden temperature fluctuations or environmental stress that compromises the fish's immune system.
#### Actionable Solution
- Gradually increase the water temperature to 30°C (86°F) for 10 days to speed up the parasite’s lifecycle and kill heat-sensitive theronts.
- Transfer symptomatic fish to a quarantine tank and apply a salt (NaCl) concentration of 3–5 ppt for prolonged immersion.
- For short-term "dip" treatments, use 10–20 ppt for 10–30 minutes under close supervision to disrupt the osmotic balance of parasites.

### Columnaris (Cotton Wool Disease)
#### Symptoms
- Clinical signs include yellowish-white, "cotton-like" patches on the head, mouth, and scales.
- A classic "saddleback" lesion often appears around the dorsal fin; advanced cases show frayed fins and extensive gill necrosis (brownish discoloration).
#### Root Cause / Pathogen
- Caused by the Gram-negative, aerobic bacterium *Flavobacterium columnare*.
- An opportunistic pathogen that becomes virulent under high organic loading, low dissolved oxygen, overcrowding, and temperatures exceeding 25°C (77°F).
#### Actionable Solution
- Lower the water temperature to below 25°C (77°F) to reduce the growth rate of the bacteria.
- Perform significant water exchanges and increase aeration to reduce organic matter and ammonia levels.
- Use Potassium Permanganate (KMnO4) **ONLY** in an isolated hospital tank at a dosage of 2 mg/L for a 4–8 hour bath (ensure water remains pink).

### Streptococcus in Tilapia
#### Symptoms
- Infected tilapia typically display "pop-eye" (exophthalmia) with associated hemorrhaging, a distended abdomen (dropsy), and darkened skin.
- Neurological symptoms are common, including erratic swimming or "whirling" behavior.
#### Root Cause / Pathogen
- Bacterial infection primarily caused by *Streptococcus agalactiae* or *S. iniae*.
- Outbreaks are highly correlated with chronic stress and high water temperatures (above 28°C / 82°F) which favor rapid bacterial replication.
#### Actionable Solution
- Implement strict quarantine for all new fish and lower system temperature to below 27°C (81°F).
- Immediately remove and humanely euthanize any fish displaying "whirling" or exophthalmia to prevent further shedding of bacteria.
- Reduce feeding rates during an outbreak to minimize metabolic stress and waste production.

### Monogenean Flukes (Gill and Skin Flukes)
#### Symptoms
- Infested fish show heavy breathing (gasping at the surface), flared opercula (gill covers), and excessive mucus production on the skin or gills.
- Fish appear lethargic and may exhibit pale gills due to tissue damage and localized hemorrhaging.
#### Root Cause / Pathogen
- Parasitic flatworms, primarily *Dactylogyrus* (gill flukes) and *Gyrodactylus* (skin flukes), which use hooked haptors to graze on host tissues.
#### Actionable Solution
- Transfer affected fish to a quarantine tank for salt baths: 10–30 ppt for 10–30 minutes, or 3–5 ppt for continuous exposure over several days.
- Increase aeration in the main system to 5–8 mg/L to help fish compensate for gill damage.
- Always quarantine new stock for at least 2 weeks, as flukes are often introduced via asymptomatic carriers.
